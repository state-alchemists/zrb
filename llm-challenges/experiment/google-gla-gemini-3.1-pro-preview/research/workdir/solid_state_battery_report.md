# Solid State Battery (SSB) Status Update: Late 2024 / 2025

## 1. When are they actually hitting the market (cars)?

The transition to solid-state batteries is happening gradually, shifting from "semi-solid" stepping stones to fully solid architectures. Here is the realistic timeline as of late 2024/2025:

*   **Late 2024 (Semi-Solid Phase):** We have already seen the introduction of "semi-solid-state" batteries in the market. Chinese automakers like **SAIC Motor** and **GAC** rolled out initial vehicles utilizing semi-solid batteries (which still use some liquid electrolyte and traditional anode materials) as a stepping stone.
*   **2025 - 2026 (Pilots and Premium/Niche Vehicles):** This period marks the start of limited commercial deployment.
    *   **Mercedes-Benz** is expected to integrate next-generation silicon-anode batteries (via Sila) into the electric G-Wagon around 2025/2026.
    *   Motorcycles are acting as early testbeds. For example, Verge Motorcycles plans to deploy SSBs in their TS Pro and Ultra models by Q1 2026, and the Volkswagen-QuantumScape partnership recently showcased a prototype battery in a Ducati race bike.
    *   Manufacturers like **Solid Power** and **Nissan** are opening pilot production facilities in 2025.
*   **2027 - 2029 (Broad Mass Production):** This is the window where *fully* solid-state batteries are expected to hit the mainstream automotive market. 
    *   **Toyota** is targeting 2027 to launch its first EV powered by a fully solid-state battery.
    *   **Nissan** has officially set 2028 as its target for mass production.
    *   **Panasonic** and **SK** are aiming for commercialization by 2029.

## 3. What are the main technical problems they are still trying to solve?

Despite moving out of the lab and into pilot plants, engineers are still battling several fundamental physical and manufacturing hurdles:

*   **The "Solid-Solid" Interface Issue:** In traditional batteries, liquid electrolytes easily wet the electrodes, ensuring continuous electrical contact. In SSBs, two hard solids must touch. They often only make contact in small patches, which creates high internal electrical resistance. Companies are trying to solve this by mixing materials to lower the required pressure between the layers from megapascals down to kilopascals.
*   **Lithium Dendrites (Filament Growth):** SSBs aim to use lithium-metal anodes to vastly increase energy density. However, during charging, microscopic lithium fibers (dendrites) can grow, fracture the solid electrolyte due to stress gradients, and cause a short circuit. 
*   **Manufacturing Scale and Low Yields:** Making SSBs requires entirely new manufacturing equipment and global supply chains. Solid electrolytes must be manufactured at incredibly thin dimensions (e.g., 20 microns thick). Maintaining quality control at high speeds is currently resulting in low yields and high defect rates.
*   **Cost:** Because production has not yet scaled, SSBs remain prohibitively expensive. Estimates suggest solid-state batteries currently cost roughly $400 to $800 per kWh, compared to about $115 per kWh for standard Lithium-ion batteries.
*   **Thermal Runaway in Extreme Conditions:** While SSBs are widely touted as safer than liquid Li-ion batteries, recent extreme testing (like nail penetration or extrusion) on certain sulfide-based solid-state batteries still resulted in massive heat generation and thermal runaway.
